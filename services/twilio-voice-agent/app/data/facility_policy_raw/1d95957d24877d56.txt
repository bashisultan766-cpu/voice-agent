3006. Contraband. 
Incarcerated persons  may possess only the personal property, materials, supplies, items, 
commodities and substances, up to the maximum amount, received or obtained from authorized 
sources, as permitted in these regulations. Possession of contraband as defined in section 3000 
may result in disciplinary action and confiscation of the contraband. 
 
(a) Dangerous Property. Incarcerated persons shall not possess or have under their control or 
constructive possession any weapons, explosives, explosive making material, poisons, or any 
destructive devices, nor shall they possess or assist in circulating any writing or voice recording 
which describes the making of any weapons, explosives, poisons , or destructive devices. 
Incarcerated persons shall not possess wireless communication devices capable of making or 
receiving wireless communications, except as expressly authorized by the Secretary, pursuant 
to subsection 3190(k)(8)(l)(7). 
(b) Money. Incarcerated persons may shall not possess money. If an incarcerated person finds 
money and voluntarily surrenders it, and the rightful owner does not claim it within 30 days, it will 
shall be credited to the incarcerated person’s trust account. 
(c) Except as authorized by the institution head, incarcerated persons shall not possess or have 
under their control any matter which contains or concerns any of the following: 
(1) Any matter of a character tending to incite murder; arson; riot; or any form of violence or 
physical harm to any person, or any ethnic, gender, racial, religious, or other group. 
(2) Blackmail or extortion. 
(3) Contraband, or sending or receiving contraband. 
(4) Plans to escape or assist in an escape. 
(5) Plans to disrupt the order, or breach the security, of any facility institution. 
(6) Plans for activities which violate the law, these regulations, or local procedures. 
(7) Coded messages. 
(8) A description of the making of any weapon, explosive, poison or destructive device. 
(9) Illustrations, explanations, and/or descriptions of how to sabotage or disrupt computers, 
communications, or electronics. 
(10) Diskettes.  
(11) Catalogs, advertisements, brochures, and other commercial material which are obscene in 
nature as described in subsection (15) below subsections 3006(c)(15)-(16). 
(12) Maps depicting any area within a ten 10-mile radius of an facility institution. 
(13) Gambling or a lottery. 
(14) Markings on the envelope which are obscene in nature as described in subsection (c)(15) 
below or sexually explicit in nature as described in subsection (c)(16).

Chapter 1. Rules and Regulations of Adult Operations and Programs 
Subchapter 2. Incarcerated Persons Resources 
Article 4. Mail 
 
3130. General Policy.  
The California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation (CDCR) encourages 
correspondence between i ncarcerated per sons and persons outside the correctional facility 
institution. The sending and receiving of mail by incarcerated persons shall be uninhibited except 
as specifically provided for in this article. The Rregulations contained in this article shall provide 
for the orderly processing of incarcerated person mail and to give direction to staff, incarcerated 
persons, and their correspondents concerning facility institution mail requirements. Mail shall be 
delivered to incarcerated persons, regardless of housing, unless it is contraband pursuant to 
section 3006, is not authorized pursuant to section 3134 , or is determined to be  disturbing or 
Ooffensive Correspondence pursuant to section 3135. 
 
NOTE: Authority cited: Section 5058, Penal Code. Reference: Sections 2600, 2601 (c) (d) and 
5054, Penal Code; and Procunier v. Martinez, 416 U.S. 396.  
 
3131. Plan of Operation.  
Each warden or head of a correctional facility institution shall prepare and maintain a plan of 
operation for the sending and receiving of mail for all incarcerated persons housed in the facility 
institution. The mail  pProcedures of the correctional facility  shall conform to departmental 
policies, regulations and relevant provisions of law , made reference to  and shall apply to all 
incarcerated persons of the facility institution. Correctional staff shall promptly inform each newly 
received incarcerated persons of all department regulations and local procedures governing 
incarcerated person mail. 
 
NOTE: Authority cited: Section 5058, Penal Code. Reference: Section 2080, Penal Code; 
and Procunier v. Martinez, 416 U.S. 396.

3132. Responsibility and Compliance.  
(a) Correspondents are personally responsible for the content of each item of mail they send 
into or out of a correctional facility institution. All  incarcerated persons  and persons 
corresponding with incarcerated persons must shall comply with existing laws, regulations, and 
local rules. Any violation of laws governing mail will shall be referred to postal authorities and to 
appropriate criminal prosecuting authorities, as appropriate. Violations of law, the policies and 
regulations set forth in this article, or of approved facility institution mail procedures may result 
in the temporary suspension or denial of correspondence mail between the persons involved. 
(b) Departmental employees, incarcerated persons , and persons corresponding with 
incarcerated persons  must shall comply with the regulations set forth in this article and with 
approved facility institution mail procedures. Failure to do so may result in legal or administrative 
measures against the person or persons involved. 
 
NOTE: Authority cited: Section 5058, Penal Code. Reference: Sections 2601(c), 2930, 5054 and 
5058, Penal Code; and Procunier v. Martinez, 416 U.S. 396. 
 
3133. Definitions and Disposition of Mail.  
(a) Definitions: For the purpose of this Article, mail is defined by  the following definitions shall 
apply: 
(1) First-Class Mail is all mail wholly or partly in writing or typewriting includes all handwritten or 
typewritten material, all actual and personal correspondence mail, and all personalized business 
correspondence mail bills and statements of account, and all matter sealed or otherwise closed 
against inspection . First-Class Mail includes, but is not limited to, a postcard, a standard 
envelope, or a large envelope. First -Class Mail does not include boxes  or padded envelopes, 
where the padding cannot be removed before delivery to the incarcerated person. The maximum 
weight for a First -Class letter is 13 ounces. All First -Class Mail shall be delivered to the  
incarcerated person as soon as possible, but not later than seven calendar days from receipt of 
the mail at the facility institution mailroom., unless disapproved in accordance with section 3136 
or as otherwise provided in this Article. 
 
(2) Standard Marketing Mail, formerly called  Standard Mail or Bulk Mail, is used for  includes 
advertising mail advertisements, catalogues, and solicitations of donations, newsletters and mail 
of a non-personal nature that are not required to be mailed as First -Class Mail. The maximum 
weight for Standard Marketing Mail is 16 ounces. 
(3) Periodicals are a class of mail consisting of magazines, newspapers , or newsletters, and 
other publications formed of printed sheets that are published at least four times a year at 
regular, specified intervals  from a known office of publication. The known office of publication 
must be a public office for transacting the business of the publication during normal business 
hours, and must also be the office where the publication's circulation records are available for 
USPS examination. All periodicals shall be delivered to the incarcerated person no later than 
fifteen (15) calendar days from receipt of the mail at the institution mailroom, unless disapproved 
in accordance with section 3134.1. 
(A) All periodicals shall be delivered to the incarcerated person, as soon as possible,  no later 
than fifteen (15) calendar days from receipt of the mail at the institution mailroom, unless 
disapproved in accordance with section 3134.1 or as otherwise provided in this Article. 
(B) Except as otherwise authorized by the department, incarcerated persons  may receive  
periodicals directly from bookstores, book distributors, publishers that conduct mail order

(b) Disposition of Mail. All incoming and outgoing mail shall be handled in accordance with the

(1) All incoming mail shall be properly addressed. Appropriately Properly addressed incoming

mail should also include the address designated by the  institution for incarcerated person mail.

Requested”. Staff may dispo se of any Standard Mail piece that does not have a Mailer 
Endorsement, and is undeliverable because the incarcerated person is not currently housed at

correspondence mail for personal non -business correspondence communications, the 
transmission distribution of contraband  items, or the smuggling of letters mail and or other 
communications to be forwarded to persons not listed in subsectio n(c) is an abuse of this right  
and such proven abuse may be subject to disciplinary action as described in S sections 3314 
and 3315.

confidential correspondents will shall be processed regardless of weight or postage class.

item is determined to contain contraband as described in sections 3006, 3134 or 3135, staff shall 
deliver the mail to the incarcerated person within 30 calendar days of the notice, regardless of

Response 5A: The department acknowledges the Commenter’s opposition to the

Regulations and the Initial Statement of Reasons, the department will continue with this 
rulemaking action.  
Under the current regulations, subsection 3006(c)(15)(D) provided that “text-only material 
shall not be considered obscene unless designated by the Division of Adult Institutions 
(DAI).” Although this language has been removed from the proposed regulations, it s 
inclusion in the regulations was unnecessary as any publications that contain potentially 
objectionable text will ultimately not be withheld from an inmate without a determination 
by DAI, as indicated in both the historic and proposed regulations under the provisions of 
section 3134.1. 
This regulatory package makes significant revisions to the department’s processing of 
sexual materials to provide improved clarity for staff, inmates, and the public. Subsections 
3006(c)(15) and (c)(16) have been restructured to distinguish between prohibited sexual 
material in text and prohibited sexual material in images.  
Commenter raises an example of the department potentially not permitting an inmate to 
possess written text of a legal analysis of a court case involving a conviction for sexual 
conduct. Although this hypothetical result is possible under  both the current  and 
proposed regulations, the department emphasizes that under subsection 3006(c)(15)(A) 
it is required to consider whether the text, taken as a whole, lacks any “serious literary, 
artistic, political, or scientific value.” In all likelihood, a written analysis of a court case will 
be determined to have political value.  
Finally, the 2007 PLN Settlement Agreement (Prison Legal News v. Schwarzenegger, 
No. Civ-07-02058 CW), does not provide a definition of obscene or sexual material.  
Comment 5B: Commenter is requesting the language below be added to ensure the 
definition of Obscene Text is appropriately confined: 
“Material that otherwise would qualify as Obscene Text may not be rejected or censored 
if it is commonly considered to constitute a well -known and widely recognized religious, 
academic, medical, or literary work, if it is part of a news article and the con tent is 
reasonably necessary to understand the events reported therein, or if it quotes from a 
judicial decision and the content is reasonably necessary to understand the fundamental 
legal issue or if it is legal material. Legal material includes law-related books, law-related 
documents, law-related publications, and court transcripts including, for example, court 
transcripts, court documents, Black’s Law Dictionary , The California Prison and Parole 
Law Handbook by the Prison Law Office, California Codes, Human Rights Defense 
Center publications, and official reports of case law (i.e., California Reporters).” 
 
Response 5B: The department declines the Commenter’s suggestion. California Penal

Comment 24A: Commenter represents the Office of the State Public Defender, and 
states the following: 
 
In its current proposed language California Code of Regulations Title 15 § 3138(h) states 
“indigent inmates shall have free and unlimited mail to any court or the Attorney General's 
Office.” CDCR eliminated a corresponding right for indigent inmates to hav e free and

unlimited mail access to their own lawyers. This creates the bizarre situation where an 
indigent individual has free and unlimited access to the prosecutor prosecuting them and 
the judge judging them, but not to their attorney who is guaranteed to them und er the 
state and federal constitutions. The reasoning for this decision is laid out on page 26 of 
the addendum to ISOR-NCR 23-12, noting that because not everyone is represented by 
an institutional public defender or the Office of the State Public Defender , “The addition 
of … legal entities would be impossible for the staff processing outgoing mail to identify, 
and therefore cannot be accommodated.” As a result of this stated concern CDCR 
concludes that indigent inmates simply cannot have free and unlimited access to defense 
counsel. 
  
But limiting communication with counsel is hardly the only, or even most reasonable, way 
of solving this important access issue. CDCR already has a section in CCR Title 15 that 
requires CDCR staff to review the recipients of outgoing mail and make certain 
determinations. Subsection 31411 informs CDCR staff which people and entities 
incarcerated individuals have the right to confidentially communicate with. CDCR staff 
review the outgoing correspondence to determine if the addressee meets the criteria for 
confidential communications.  
 
CDCR can simply modify § 3138(h) to read: “Indigent inmates shall have free and 
unlimited confidential mail to correspond with persons and groups identified in § 3141(c).” 
This will require no additional labor by CDCR staff. CDCR staff are already required  to 
review outgoing mail for § 3141(c) addressees, so the only thing required of them under 
the new rule will be to post the letter at no cost to the incarcerated individual.  
 
In the alternative, § 3138(h) could be modified to add: “Indigent inmates shall also have 
free and unlimited confidential mail to correspond with an attorney at law, on active status 
or otherwise eligible to practice law, listed with a state bar association and legitimate legal 
service organizations that consist of an established group of attorneys involved in the 
representation of offenders in judi cial proceedings .” The italicized language is taken 
directly from § 3141(c)(6) and § 3141(c)(10) and thus is a lready familiar to CDCR staff 
who process confidential mail using these criteria regularly. Again, CDCR staff are 
already required to review outgoing mail for this, so the only additional thing required of 
them under the new rule will be to post the letter at no cost to the incarcerated individual.  
 
While OSPD firmly believes that one of the above two solutions are reasonable and 
practical for CDCR to implement, a final alternative is to provide each eligible inmate with 
an additional 20 indigent envelopes per month for confidential communication as 
permitted in subsection 3141.2. This last option, while more limited in scope, recognizes 
that no individual should have to choose between communicating with their family 
members and communicating with legal counsel.  
Increasing the ability of indigent inmates to communicate freely on legal matters is 
important and CDCR is right to look for more opportunities to permit such access. But 
writing a letter to the courthouse or the attorney general is no substitute for communicating 
confidentially and freely with your attorney or seeking legal assistance. It is imperative to 
provide adequate opportunities for both personal and legal communication to every

person in California custody regardless of their ability to pay. We urge CDCR to adopt 
one of the recommendations in this letter.

 Response 24A: See response 7B. CDCR disagrees that the existing regulations, which 
allow indigent inmates to send unlimited and free mail to any court or the Attorney 
General's Office are insufficient to address the inmate’s right to access the courts. The 
historic version of subsection 3138(h) provides indigent inmates with the ability to send 
free and unlimited mail to the courts and the Attorney General’s Office to accommodate 
situations where an inmate is representing themselves in a legal ma tter and the inmate 
communicates with the court or opposing counsel (i.e., the Attorney General’s Office) 
regarding the matter. CDCR believes an inmate’s attorney was not included in the historic 
version of subsection 3138(h) because an inmate’s attorney, having their own financial 
resources for any necessary communications with the court or opposing counsel could 
do so on behalf of the inmate. To the extent an inmate’s attorney and the inmate need to 
communicate, the inmate’s attorney can send pre-paid envelopes to the inmate or make 
phone calls or visits with the inmate. CDCR also expects the inmate’s attorney can 
recuperate the cost of written communications through a variety of sources, including 
government funding. 
 
CDCR also disagrees with Commenter’s statement that to allow indigent inmates 
unlimited and free confidential mail to correspond with persons and groups identified in § 
3141(c) as confidential correspondents would not be an increased workload on mailroom 
staff. Commenter is correct that when  processing outgoing confidential mail, mailroom 
staff confirm that the addressee meets the criteria as a confidential correspondent. If 
CDCR were to allow indigent inmates to send free and unlimited mail to these 
addressees, mailroom staff would need to take the additional step of confirming with the 
institutional Accounting Office that the sending inmate was designated as indigent. As an 
alternative, Commenter suggests providing indigent inmates with 20 additional indig ent 
envelopes per month. Although CDCR appreciates Commenter’s suggestions, CDCR 
does not have the budget for an increase in the allocation of indigent envelopes.  CDCR 
is not aware of indigent inmates requesting additional envelopes.

Response 26C:  See response s 9A and 11B . The department recognizes the 
Commenter’s concerns, however ; the requirement for parolees to obtain written 
authorization to correspond has remained unchanged and is already in effect under the 
previous regulatory  text in subsections 3139(b) and 3139(c) , which state in part that 
parolees may initiate requests by contacting their Parole Agent (PA) and that the PA shall 
interview the parolee or review their Field File to obtain the information required to process 
the Request for Correspondence Approval, CDC Form 1074.  The proposed language to 
subsection 3139(c) clarifies that parolees are only required to obtain approval when 
corresponding with inmates under the jurisdiction of CDCR.  Further, the department has 
added language which states “Except as otherwise authorized by the department…”.  
This additional language provides for exceptions when deemed necessary by the 
department.  An example may include cases where correspondence is required as part 
of in-prison programming, as outlined in the comment above. With regard to Commenter’s 
concern regarding the workload for parole admin istrators and supervisors and concerns 
that the review of parolee correspondence requests will not be processed timely, that has 
not been CDCR’s experience. A typical parole office only handles a handful of parolee 
requests to correspond with inmates each year, which is much less than, by comparison, 
the number of requests for travel passes processed by a typical parole office. CDCR also 
disagrees with Commenter’s claim “[t]he proposed regulations also create an additional 
basis for parole violations, which could cause unnecessary and life -altering re -
incarceration simply because people on parole were unaware of this new leg al 
requirement for correspondence.” For purposes of due process, if a parolee is found to 
have corresponded with an inmate without prior approval, the parole agent’s decision 
whether to violate parole would be based on the individual circumstances of the parolee, 
including the parolee’s prior knowledge of the prohibitio n. CDCR also emphasizes that 
parole violations are progressive, meaning that depending upon the severity of the 
violation and other individualized circumstances, there are many levels of reme dial 
sanctions short of pursuing a petition to a court to return the parolee to incarceration.

Response 27A: See Responses 9A, 11B and  26C. With regard to Commenter’s claim 
that section 3139 exceeds CDCR’s statutory authority, CDCR responds that Penal Code 
section 3000 et seq. provide CDCR the authority to supervise specified individuals on 
parole. Parole is the conditional release of an individual from custody, and the parolee 
remains under the ongoing supervision of the parole authority until such time as parole 
ends or is discharged. C ase law supports CDCR’s authority to place restrictions on a 
parolee’s freedom, recognizing that a parolee may be subject to restrictions not applicable 
to other citizens. (See Morrissey v. Brewer (1972) 408 U.S. 471.)
